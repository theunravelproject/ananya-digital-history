# Journal A

Discussing digital history is undeniably important in our current place in the digital age. I have found thorugh my own preconceptions of the concept of digital history, along with Douglas Seefeldt and William Thomans' definiton of digital history [2] that there is a new reality, or perhaps dimension, created by the digital space that is aptly reflected through Histrians' ongoing efforts to digitize history. Discussions of crossing boundaries of tradition, and addressing tension between maintaining historical integrity and expanding toward new innovations. This was my first time using Github, so navigating the space and creating new repositories was definitely a learning curve! I honestly really enjoy the layout and interface of the website and I hope to increase my comfort with the medium as we navigate through the course. I have coded before, but only in teh statistics realm, so navigating Github, I realized that I still had a lot of preconcieved notions of what using more advanced technological interfaces would be like. I was a little daunted by the idea of coding, because of my experiences with R. I feel that my nerves relate heavily to chapter one of "Technology and the Historian: Transformations in the Digital Age By: Adam Crymble. I found that I had a misconception that technology was going to be and entirely daunting, expertise-driven "seamless continuom"[3] that I would need to hunker down to learn if I wanted to keep up with the constantly evolving realm of Humanities. In truth, coming at these concepts from a post-moderm Metaphysics background, I can see immediately that there is much more to the discussion of presence, ethics, and progressions within History. I terally enjoyed Benjamin Schmidt's explanaition of Technology being constructed through grey-scale, rather than black-and-white thinking. [4] He thouroughly understood that there is much more nuance and ambiguity possible through technology than we commonly understand. Yet importantly, it remains a tool and not a replacement for human involvement, which I find to be one of the most important conversations surrounding technology within the Humanities. It closely relates to the understandings of a new dimension entering the human sphere. There are countless complexities to the presence of digital spaces, yet with great acknowledgement and reacknowledgement of humanity, it seems to hold a great potential for the future. I am excited to learn Github, create a website, and construct my first digital History project.

Working with OpenRefine this week was pretty interesting. I found that cleaning data felt a bit intangible. I knew that it was important and necessary work, which would of course have a larger impact on the dataset and it's accessibility, yet each refinement that I did seemed hard to track or double check. It seems obvious that the sheer quantity of data that has been opened to single-person-management and accessibility through digitization is far more than previously imagiable. I found that my thoughts on the matter were extremely reminiscent of Kristen Nawrotzki and Jack Dougherty's piece "Writing History in the Digital Age," (the larger text which housed Ansley T. Erickson's "Reflections on 10,000 Digital notecards) where their discussion of The Digital Revolution's transformation of the past held massive impllications not only on the process of the historian, but the larger way in which human beings thinks, process, teach, and communicate as a whole. Addressing massive datasets with media much larger than I could have worked with in an analog sense, immensely complicated my perception of the data at hand. For the most part, we were working with artifacts, each with a complex and rich story, history, and implication, yet I could not name a single artifact or any of their names involved. On the other hand, I could feel the weightage of the potential to accesse and engage with such artifacts, along with opening the possibility to the general public. I am extremely interested in the sense of expansion to our realms of thought and therein our worlds, and working on such a large, and for myself, unintuitive, process of cleaning the data sprouted countless implications. In Erickson's piece, where she entailed her process of keeping thousands of notes on her excursions to various archives, I thought deeply about the capacity of organization of our own thoughts and experiences, essentially externalizing parts of the mind to increase capacity, efficiency, exactitude. There are already innumerable conversations within the digital history realm and the humanities as a whole which discuss the changing of the human mind, the benefits as entailed by Erickson, and the setbacks, relevantly the loss of cognition without exercising out own memories or critical thinking skills. I have long admired Philip Butler's Black Transhuman Liberation Theology where he dives into how 'Techne' assist in the human expansion of the self outside of our physical bodies. Transhuman Liberation Theology is an undoubtedly expansive conversation, yet working with OpenRefine brought up specific connections to his notions of liberation through techne, in this case, liberation of knowledge. The theological aspect was also more than apt, where the sense of overwhelm or perhaps existential dread, was almost reminiscent of religion. There was a sense of power to 'cleaning' such data for masses to possibly access and engage with, we were expanding the realm of knowldge singlehandedly (of course, with the assistance of technology), that literally extended out own capability outside of our bodies. I am more than excited to discuss the topic further in class and explore more media on the topics at hand!


# Journal D

Working with RAWGraphs 2.0 was extremely helpful with navigating digital visualization design for some of the data that Syd and I will be working with for our final project. After reading Aimee Knight’s “Where Data Meets Design” I had been thinking a lot about the communicability of data. There is a sense of importance in visualizing data in the digital world. As we work so closely with massive, complicated, and unintuitive data sets, visualization becomes a sort of translator, as Knight explains, to revitalize the humanity in our information. [1]

The process began with organizing the data into a spreadsheet, separating the information into categories that we thought would be relevant for viewers to showcase. The data was separated by date, where it was sourced, the category of the project (Cold War/Digital Age), the group of documents, and the link to the source. These were initial categories that we felt could be relevant, yet when it actually came to visualization, they quickly changed. We had initially planned to do a beeswarm plot, to show very simply when and from where the clusters of released documents where situated, but after actually loading the data into RAWGraphs, the cluster was neither accessible nor appealing to the eye. It was clear that because we were working with more data on more of a qualitative basis, not quantitative, the graph did not work in our favor. In Knight’s “Where Data Meets Design,” one of the most relevant points in her 5 modes of inquiry was “datactors” where she clearly explained the importance of pushing data beyond just being “beautiful information” and farther into the deliverable, with the intention of creating change in the world. [2]

The website itself was extremely intuitive and easy to navigate, with loading data through the downloaded .csv/.tsv and immediately being able to play with each of the values and axes, it was simple to see how each possible visualization would display for unfamiliar viewers. There is also a very simple navigation possibility for choosing between the styles of visualization; in the end, we chose the Alluvial diagram, which shows proportional correlations between the different categories. Ultimately, with the amount and relevance of qualitative aspects of the data, it was the most ideal to display the data in its correlation to the source that owns it and the category that it fit. In theory, it allows viewers to draw conclusions about the intentionality of which documents from which time periods may be released by the government and begin to display a “visual thesis” through our data, similarly to how to Church and Hepworth describe the impartance of visual theses in “Racism in the Machine: Visualization Ethics in Digital Humanities Projects [3]. For example, the government is loosely shown have been releasing most of the primary documents from the cold war period which was more than 50 years ago and of a time for which the current administration is no longer accountable for, or could perhaps serve modern themes pushed in the public imagination about that time.

# Journal E

My digital history project episode, “PSYOPtics,” involves a good amount of text analysis of government, civilian, and scholarly texts. Working with Voyant this past week, I found that it was an extremely well-made and helpful tool that could be immensely useful in my project. With Voyant’s capability to scan documents and categorize words based on their frequencies across time, I saw immediately how there was so much potential for visual theses. Of course, we examined inaugural speeches across time, so the connections that I was drawing were based on the politics of the time and political views of each president but there was a lot to notice about the use of specific words, what they might be intending to convey, and how much emphasis has been placed on them. The Program also allows for categorizing certain words into designated categories, for example positive words were highlighted in green and negative in red. Each word shows its usage over time through a line graph that can be overlaid with other words to see the relation between the two.

When using the tool, I immediately thought of Stephen Ramsay’s “The Hermeneutics of Screwing Around,”[1] which we read for Monday’s seminar. Firstly, tools such as Voyant are of great use when addressing quantities of information that are much too large to analyze by hand (as Frank Zappa said, "So many books, so little time”), especially based on the quantitative measures of certain words across multiple documents. Being able to view the data already categorized through the program allows us, the researcher, to draw connections, propose new questions, and theorize on a deeper level about the content at hand. Secondly, I immediately partook in what Ramsay explained as “screwing around,” and productively at that. As he entails, we as human beings have an inclination to serendipitously browse, absorb, and engage with material with no question that we desire to answer, and that can be invaluable to creating questions. With Voyant, I saw the frequency of words that I would have never sought out of a text, but were now much more relevant to me through their progression, whether they had lost frequency, gained frequency, or remained consistently frequent throughout the presidencies. I saw words that peaked during times of war or with particular presidents, that prompted countless questions, assumptions, and theses. 

I think using the tool to scan documents that Syd and I are currently working with could display relevant patterns across types of media (government, civilian, etc.) and categorized time periods (Cold War and Digital Age). I immediately thought about the research capability of using the tool right now to prompt a deeper exploration of America’s public imagination as it has been sculpted and effected by psychological operations. A large facet of PSYOPs involves language choice and very intentional curation of themes to convey messages for the purpose of persuasion, so working with a data mining tool like Voyant, allows a much clearer display of data that would even be extremely valuable to be available to the public for their own viewing, perhaps as an add-on to the Digital Collections tab. 

The more clerical aspect of the ideating though, was in the quality of the data that we have. As Matthew Jockers and Ted Underwood mentioned in “Text Mining the Humanities,” although data mining is extremely important, one important challenge to note is about the data quality and imperfection bias [2]. This became all the more relevant to me when realizing that a large majority of our documents were not OCR compatible, or that the formerly classified documents were scans that were not immediately machine-readable. In terms of using tools like Voyant, that meant that we were going to have to use software to process the documents to be readable. I then went back in to note how much of certain documents involved handwriting and other ambiguities, and noted that there was a great amount of room for error with our documents. As a part of our broadcast manifesto, transparency, accessibility, and truth are all crucial for a modern digital humanities project, and making sure that our data is correct and reflective of the actual documents that we have will be just as important. With help from Dr. Carty, I compiled the available Github tools for converting the documents to OCR compatible ones, and I plan to troubleshoot the hand-written portions if we move forward with mining the texts with Voyant.

For the workshop this week, I chose to work with QGIS because of its extended capacities with spatial mapping. In order to work with it, first I had to download the whole package for QGIS 4.3, then download the maps from the tutorial. Only 4 out of 6 of them were actually available to be downloaded. Then once I loaded them up, I was able to unzip them, and paste them directly into QGIS. I encountered more than a few technical difficulties throughout the process. The first and most prominent was that as soon as I loaded the layers of the map, I tried to zoom in and because of a mac os blip with QGIs, the program would only zoom out. Then, because I had not yet specified my coordinates, I actually lost the map entirely and could not reset the positioning again! I ended up having to delete the whole project and reload the maps. Then, as I went through the tutorial, I was able to fix and set extremely detailed aspects of the map, making it more user-friendly and tailored to the uses that I was hoping for. Throughout the tutorial, I was able to become more comfortable with the available features of QGIS and hope to use it in future projects. 

I personally really appreciate the abstract, it ties in extremely closely to my concentration in metaphysics, yet it was a notable point that the seminar this week was much more abstract than previous ones that we have had in our course. I did find that through navigating and directly running QGIS, I was able to materialize some of the more abstract concepts that we touched on this past Tuesday. In Richard White’s “What is Spatial History,” where he described Henri Lefebvre’s 3 styles of space: 
Spatial practice: separate[segregated] constructed realms of space linked by human movement
Representations of space: the physical attempt to conceive space in order to shape what is lived and perceived. [powerful and hopeless]
Representational space: space as it is lived and experienced through a set of symbolic associations. Overlays physical space and makes symbolic use of its physical objects. [makes it so that it is what religious people feel in a religious space. 

I found that the layers of the map, the representations of space were specifically catering to the spatial practice, they were being used to directly shape what was experienced by users who are able to conceptualize their space based on the various layers of this map. One the one hand, to view a space based on the uniform human-constructed lots in comparison to the same area of land based on the various water networks displays a completely different space, neither of which are truly present in the representational space. Our physical bodies do not intuitively experience space in that conceptualized way, but I am sure that interacting with such a map has a great effect on how the land is conceptualized and therefore experienced. It was ultimately quite interesting to navigate the multiple facets of spatial conceptualization and experience. It really made me wonder about how this has shown up in my own experience of the world. I often think about the fact that I have an understanding of navigating my own life, that is sometimes directly conceptualized through my google-maps perception of the area that i live in, in comparison to my job for example, or to school, even campus itself! Although I am physically in space, I still see things in my head as the map that I use most often, which fails to account for the water ways, the green spaces, and various other aspects that would be just as relevant to my experience of the world. This only prompted branches of further thought on the political, phenomenological, and temporal implications of these relationships. Ultimately navigating QGIS, along with learning a technical skill, helped me to further nuance my conceptualizations of space, and understand the sheer power of spatial representations in our lives.
